\section{Business Processes}
Organisations divide labour into \textbf{specialised work units}, each efficient at a narrow range of goods or services. The principle is \textbf{Taylorism} (Frederick Taylor): break a process into atomic units and assign each to a worker who gains deep competence in one operation but loses sight of the whole. Management plans, workers execute and receive orders rather than explanations, and \textbf{time-and-motion studies} fix the ``one best way'' for each task.

The specialist is faster and more accurate than a generalist, but becomes replaceable and needs the organisation to coordinate their output. This fits manufacturing, where assembly-line tasks are self-contained, and fails on \textbf{knowledge work}, where steps are interdependent and a worker must grasp the whole case to decide soundly. Handovers become the bottleneck: not for the time lost between desks, but for the context that fails to travel with the work. Workers who never see how their task serves a customer lose motivation, and with it productivity.

\textbf{Process orientation} shifts focus from isolated tasks to connected chains of activity, combining small units into larger ones: a worker handling a complete sub-process sees fewer handovers and keeps the context to decide within their scope. It makes the flow of work visible across the organisation and challenges the old hierarchy.

\subsection{Organisational Structures and Actors}

Organisations allocate work to resources (people, machines, or groups) under constraints of competence and authority. An \textbf{organisational structure} formalises that allocation, classifying resources by \textbf{functions}, the competencies and specialised knowledge they possess, and \textbf{roles}, the position held within the organisation, which defines rights, duties, and place in the hierarchy. A bank teller has the function to run routine transactions but not the authority to approve loans. A single resource can hold multiple roles simultaneously or in sequence: a senior accountant may serve both as a practitioner (reviewing ledgers) and as a mentor (training junior staff).

Organisations typically adopt one of three structural models:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Network structure}: autonomous actors collaborate to produce goods or services. Relationships are non-hierarchical and dynamic. This model permits ad-hoc clustering, outsourcing, and fluid team membership, making it flexible but harder to standardise and control.

  \item \textbf{Hierarchical structure}: a \emph{rooted tree} (a connected acyclic graph with one distinguished vertex orienting all edges away from it). Internal nodes are roles and functions, leaves are staff or departments, branches are authority relationships. A clear organisation chart, but rigid and slow to adapt.

  \item \textbf{Matrix structure}: a grid crossing function and process, one column per function, one row per process. Each employee reports both to a functional manager (technical competence) and to a \textbf{process owner} coordinating the end-to-end workflow. This dual accountability is both its strength and its risk: it buys coordination across silos at the price of conflicting lines of command.
\end{itemize}

Work crosses those structures on assignment. Whoever assigns it is a \textbf{principal}: an individual, a department, or an external firm. Principals come in two kinds. A \textbf{boss} assigns work by hierarchy and authority; a \textbf{customer} requests it for compensation. The two overlap when a boss assigns work on behalf of customers. The recipient is a \textbf{contractor}, human or not, since machines and software also serve, and a \textbf{contract} formalises the agreement: the case, the deadline, the compensation, and a communication protocol that leaves room for negotiation before work starts.

An actor can hold both roles, taking work from one principal while delegating parts of it to other contractors. The resulting chain of delegation is a \textbf{contract tree}, a hierarchical decomposition of the work itself, not of the organisation: unlike the hierarchical structure above, it is built per case and dissolves when the work is done.

\subsection{Cases and Procedures}

Work comes in discrete units, each with a clear beginning and end. A \textbf{case} is a unit of work to be performed or a product to be transformed; its output may be tangible (a house) or abstract (an insurance claim).

A \textbf{procedure} specifies how to handle a case: the tasks to carry out and the conditions determining their order. It is a reusable template, applied to multiple cases. Each \textbf{task} is a logical step that runs as a single indivisible whole, and a \textbf{resource} (a person, machine, or group thereof) executes it. Some tasks run on machines alone; others need human judgement, since a bank loan officer must evaluate risk where an algorithm cannot. Either way the resource must hold the relevant \textbf{knowledge}: skills, training, experience, and familiarity with organisational guidelines.

The conditions on the order are a \textbf{precedence relation} $\prec$ on the tasks: $x \prec y$ means $x$ must be completed before $y$ starts. A single execution of the procedure lists its tasks in one order compatible with $\prec$ (a \emph{linear extension} of the relation); such a sequence is a valid \textbf{execution trace}.

\begin{examplebox}{Which orderings are valid?}
  Take $S = \{a,b,c,d,e,f\}$ with precedence $a \prec b \prec d \prec f$, $a \prec c \prec e \prec f$, and $c \prec d$. So $a$ starts, $f$ ends, $b$ and $c$ are free to swap, but $d$ waits for both. Which of these six traces respect every constraint?
  \begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{lll@{\qquad}lll}
      $abcdef$ & valid   & & $acebdf$ & valid \\
      $abcedf$ & valid   & & $acbedf$ & valid \\
      $abdcef$ & \textbf{invalid} & & $acefbd$ & \textbf{invalid} \\
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  The two invalid traces each reverse a required order: $abdcef$ puts $d$ before $c$ (violating $c \prec d$), and $acefbd$ puts $f$ before $b$ and $d$ (violating $b \prec d \prec f$).
\end{examplebox}

An \textbf{activity} is the concrete performance of a task by a specific resource in a specific case. Cases share a procedure, but each may trigger different activities depending on its attributes: two insurance claims follow the same procedure, yet one requires objection handling and the other does not.

A company runs far fewer procedures than cases, and cost per case falls as volume rises: one pattern reused a hundred times costs less per unit than a hundred bespoke designs. Hence the design goal: few well-designed procedures, each serving many cases, held against the risk that oversimplifying into one universal procedure makes it unworkable.

\subsection{Business process}

Three classic definitions state the same core, an input-to-output transformation, each stressing a different facet: \emph{Hammer \& Champy} (1993) require an output of value to the customer, \emph{Davenport} (1993) an ordering of work activities across time and space with a beginning and an end, \emph{Johansson et al.}\ (1993) a set of linked activities transforming input into output.

The focus shifts from \emph{what} is produced to \emph{how}: from the product to the business logic that yields it. The transformation must \emph{add value}, the output worth more than the inputs it consumes, and the process improves as that gap widens, whether by cutting cost or raising satisfaction. This demands clear boundaries, inputs, and outputs; structure is not the enemy of efficiency but the condition for coordinating and improving the work. A process needs a defined \textbf{owner} accountable for its end-to-end design, an authority that cuts across the functional hierarchy because processes are inherently cross-functional.

Rummler and Brache (1995) classify processes by who sees the output. \textbf{Primary} processes produce the company's goods or services for external customers, generating revenue (design, engineering, distribution). \textbf{Secondary} processes stay invisible to those customers but support the primary ones (maintenance, personnel, financial administration). \textbf{Tertiary} processes direct and coordinate both, setting objectives and allocating resources.
